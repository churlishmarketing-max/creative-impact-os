# THE SITUATION PLAYBOOKS — Every Email That Hits the Desk
**Sixteen situations. Each one: the job, the read, the structure, the skeleton, the spike-watch.**

Table of contents:
1. Inbound lead reply · 2. Cold one-off (single target) · 3. Warm follow-up after a call · 4. Ghosted proposal revival · 5. Past-client reactivation · 6. Price objection · 7. "We're going with someone else" · 8. Scope creep pushback · 9. Bad news / delay / miss · 10. Apology & recovery · 11. Collections ladder · 12. Renewal conversation · 13. Referral ask · 14. Testimonial / case-study ask · 15. Saying no / declining / parting ways · 16. Escalation & platform disputes

Register key per Desk Law §6: **Call-out** · **Counsel** · **Steel** · **Documented**.

All skeletons are structural examples — swap in the real names, numbers, and receipts from the actual situation. Never send a skeleton with the scaffolding showing.

## 1 · INBOUND LEAD REPLY — Register: Counsel, warm end
**The job:** Convert curiosity into a booked conversation before the lead cools. Speed IS the strategy (Desk Law §8).
**The read:** Where did they come from (ad, referral, Diagnostic funnel, DM)? What did they already see? Their entry point tells you what they already believe — don't re-pitch what the ad already sold.
**The structure:** Acknowledge their specific ask → one line that proves a human read it → one qualifying question OR the direct path to the next step. Never both a question and a booking ask — pick based on how qualified they arrive.
**Subject:** Reply in-thread. New thread only if they came from a non-email channel: "Your question about [their thing]"
**Skeleton:**
> Saw your note about the lead flow on the [their industry] side — the "ads run but the phone doesn't ring" version of that usually isn't an ads problem, which is the good news.
>
> Quickest way to know for sure is the Authority Diagnostic — $750, flat, and you leave with the actual answer either way.
>
> Worth 15 minutes this week to see if it fits? Reply with a day that works and I'll send times.
**Spike-watch:** Don't send a menu of services. One path. Don't reply in 48 hours with an apology for the delay — reply in 15 minutes with none needed.

## 2 · COLD ONE-OFF (SINGLE TARGET) — Register: Call-out, dialed to respect
**The job:** Open a conversation with ONE specific person worth a bespoke email — a dream client, a strategic partner, a podcast guest, a JV. This is not Falcon volume; this is a sniper round.
**The read:** What did they *do* recently that you can name specifically? A cold one-off with no observed detail is a template wearing a suit. If you can't name something real, you're not ready to send.
**The structure:** The observed detail (proof you did the work) → the specific overlap between their world and yours → one small, easy, flattering ask. Relevance before pitch, always. Under 110 words.
**Subject:** Their thing, not yours: "Your [specific project] + an idea" — never "Partnership opportunity."
**Skeleton:**
> Your [specific recent thing — episode, launch, post] landed on my desk twice in one week, which usually means something.
>
> We run Creative Impact here in Charlotte — story-driven video and authority positioning for service businesses. The overlap: [one specific, concrete way their audience/work and yours intersect].
>
> No pitch. One question: would [small specific ask — a 15-minute swap of notes, a guest slot, a look at one idea] be worth your time? If not, this email self-destructs gracefully.
**Spike-watch:** No "my name is and I'm with" openers. No listing services. The ask must cost them almost nothing to say yes to.

## 3 · WARM FOLLOW-UP AFTER A CALL — Register: Counsel
**The job:** Convert a good conversation into a decision with a deadline. The follow-up email is where deals actually close or quietly die.
**The read:** What did THEY say the problem was, in their words? What was the moment they leaned in? That moment is the email's spine.
**The structure:** Their words mirrored back (the "that's right" trigger) → the exact next step with the exact number → one date that creates a real edge (a slot, a start date, a production window — real ones only, never manufactured).
**Subject:** "[Their goal] — the plan from today's call"
**Skeleton:**
> You said it cleaner than I could: "[their exact phrase from the call]." That's the whole problem in one sentence.
>
> The plan we sketched: [offer name] — [what it produces, in outcomes]. [Price], [term]. Ad spend runs separately, minimum $500/mo.
>
> I can hold the [month] production slot through [specific date]. After that it goes to the next call on the calendar — not pressure, just how the calendar works.
>
> What would need to be true on your end for this to be a yes?
**Spike-watch:** Never re-pitch features they already heard. Never discount the first month — ever. The calibrated closing question replaces "let me know your thoughts."

## 4 · GHOSTED PROPOSAL REVIVAL — Register: Counsel, one notch drier
**The job:** Get a decision — including a no. A dead proposal costing follow-up energy is worse than a clean no.
**The read:** How long silent? One week = they're busy. Three weeks = something unspoken (usually money or an internal blocker). Six weeks = run the breakup email and free the slot.
**The structure (three-shot ladder, then done):**
- **Shot 1 (day 5–7):** New value, zero pressure. Send something useful related to their situation + one line: "Proposal's still live on my end — no rush on yours."
- **Shot 2 (day 12–14):** Label the unspoken. "It seems like something in the proposal didn't sit right — the number, the timeline, or something I missed. Which one?"
- **Shot 3 (day 21+), the breakup email:** "Have you given up on [the goal they stated] for this quarter? If so, no hard feelings — I'll close the file and the slot goes to the waitlist. If not, tell me what's actually in the way." (The no-oriented question outperforms every "still interested?" ever sent.)
**Subject:** Shot 1–2 in-thread. Shot 3: "Closing the file on [project]?"
**Spike-watch:** Never "just bumping this." Never guilt. Never a fourth shot — after the breakup email, they revive it or Luke Cage's pipeline hygiene closes it.

## 5 · PAST-CLIENT REACTIVATION — Register: Call-out softened by history
**The job:** Reopen a relationship that ended fine (non-fine endings route to Playbook 10 first). Past clients are the cheapest revenue in the book — zero acquisition cost, pre-built trust.
**The read:** Why did it actually end? What's changed at Creative Impact since (new offers, new proof) that's *relevant to their situation specifically*? What's visibly changed in their world (check their site, socials, ads — fetch before writing)?
**The structure:** A real observation about their current world → the honest reason for writing ("you came up in our [relevant thing] and I looked you up") → what's new that maps to them → low-friction re-entry (Diagnostic at $750 is the natural reactivation door — small, paid, zero-commitment diagnosis).
**Subject:** "[Their company] — what I noticed" or "It's been [N] months — one observation"
**Skeleton:**
> Went looking at your [site/channel/ads] this week — the [specific real observation, ideally a gap or a win] jumped out.
>
> Since we wrapped [past project], we built something that didn't exist then: [relevant new capability, one line]. It maps directly onto [the observed gap].
>
> Cleanest way back in, if you want one: the Authority Diagnostic — $750 flat, you get the full read on [their gap] whether or not we ever work together again.
>
> Would it be a bad idea to spend 15 minutes on it?
**Spike-watch:** Never open with "it's been a while!" nostalgia — open with their present, not your past. Never offer a discount to come back; offer *relevance*.

## 6 · PRICE OBJECTION — Register: Counsel with a steel floor
**The job:** Hold the price and move the frame. The price is never negotiated down; the *scope* can be traded, or the *entry point* can change (Diagnostic $750 → Story Capture Pilot $2,400 exist precisely as lower doors).
**The read:** Is this "can't afford it" (route to a smaller door), "won't prioritize it" (a value problem — re-anchor to the cost of the problem), or "testing whether you'll fold" (hold, warmly)? Their exact wording tells you which.
**The structure:** Label without judgment ("Sounds like $[X]/mo is landing heavier than expected") → re-anchor to the cost of not solving it, with their numbers → offer a different DOOR, never a different price → calibrated question.
**Skeleton (the "won't prioritize" variant):**
> Sounds like $3,500 a month is landing as a big number — fair.
>
> Here's the math I'd want in your seat: you told me a closed [their client type] is worth $[their number]. The Engine has to produce [small number] of those over the term to go positive. Everything past that is margin.
>
> If the monthly is the wrong shape for right now, the Story Capture Pilot is the other door — $2,400 one-time, you own everything it produces, and the Engine's there when the timing's right.
>
> Which of those two actually fits the quarter you're planning?
**Spike-watch:** Never justify the price with your costs — anchor to their outcome. Never split the difference. Never say "discount" in any direction. (Churlish's case-study-exchange offer did not port — Creative Impact has no equivalent yet; if the founders create one, it gets described as an *exchange*, never a discount.)

## 7 · "WE'RE GOING WITH SOMEONE ELSE" — Register: Counsel, gracious
**The job:** Lose the deal, keep the relationship, plant the callback. A meaningful share of "we chose someone else" decisions wobble within 90 days — this email decides whether you're the call they make.
**The structure:** Genuine congratulations, zero passive aggression → one sentence of standing value ("the [specific thing] we mapped in the proposal is yours to use regardless") → the open door with a timestamp ("if the results aren't where you want them in 90 days, the conversation's already half-done").
**Skeleton:**
> Appreciate you closing the loop — most people just go quiet, and this is classier.
>
> Genuinely hope it works. The positioning read from our proposal is yours either way; use it, it's right.
>
> One thing I'll say plainly: if 90 days in the [outcome they wanted] isn't happening, you won't have to start the conversation over. It'll be right where we left it.
>
> — Brandon
**Spike-watch:** No "you're making a mistake" energy, however disguised. No asking why (they'll tell you unprompted if the door stays open). Set a Luke Cage-style 90-day check on the file.

## 8 · SCOPE CREEP PUSHBACK — Register: Steel wrapped in Counsel
**The job:** Protect the margin without souring the room. The move is never "no" — it's "yes, and here's what that costs," delivered like a partner, not a bouncer.
**The read:** Is this a one-time small ask (sometimes goodwill IS the right call — but *named* as goodwill, so it's never invisible), a pattern forming (stop it now or it's the new normal by month three), or a signal they actually need the bigger tier?
**The structure:** Enthusiasm for the idea itself → the boundary stated as fact, not apology ("that sits outside the [tier] scope") → the two clean paths: priced as an add (invoice-scoper builds the line item) or traded against something in current scope → their pick.
**Skeleton:**
> The [requested thing] is a good idea — worth doing properly.
>
> It sits outside the Engine scope, so two clean ways to do it: I scope it as an add-on ([$X], one invoice, done right), or we trade it against [current deliverable] this month if the priority's shifted.
>
> Either works on my end. Which one matches what you actually need most this month?
**Spike-watch:** Never absorb it silently — invisible goodwill compounds into resentment and a broken P&L. Never lecture about scope; state, offer paths, ask. If it's the third add-on this quarter, the email's real job is teeing up the Market Domination conversation.

## 9 · BAD NEWS / DELAY / MISS — Register: Counsel, zero flinch
**The job:** Deliver it before they discover it, own it completely, and re-anchor confidence with a specific fix. Speed beats polish here (Desk Law §8: before they find out elsewhere, always).
**The structure:** The news in sentence one — no windup, no "so, quick update" → the honest one-line why (no essay, no excuse chain) → the fix with a date → what you're doing so it doesn't repeat → the door open for their reaction.
**Skeleton:**
> The [deliverable] is going to land [new date], not [promised date]. That's on us — [one honest line: the reason, not a story].
>
> What's already in motion: [specific fix]. You'll have [interim thing] by [date] so [their dependency] doesn't slip on your side.
>
> And so it doesn't repeat: [the process change, one line].
>
> If this creates a problem downstream for you, call me today and we'll solve that part too.
**Spike-watch:** Never bury the news under paragraph two. Never "due to unforeseen circumstances." Never promise a recovery date you're not 95% sure of — a second miss on the recovery date is how retainers die. If real money was lost, this is a **phone call first** (Desk Law §9); the email is the written confirmation after.

## 10 · APOLOGY & RECOVERY — Register: Counsel, full weight
**The job:** Rebuild trust after something genuinely went wrong. Different from bad news: the damage already landed. The apology email is judged on specificity — vague apologies read as PR.
**The structure:** Name exactly what happened, in plain words, no passive voice ("we missed X," never "X was missed") → the impact acknowledged from THEIR side of the table → what's been fixed already (past tense — action taken before the email, not promised in it) → the make-good, specific and unprompted → no ask. Recovery emails don't sell.
**Spike-watch:** No "we apologize for any inconvenience" — the phrase is a spike on sight. No explaining why it "sort of made sense at the time." No coupons dressed as make-goods; the make-good matches the damage. And no ask of any kind — the next commercial conversation happens only after trust is visibly rebuilt.

## 11 · COLLECTIONS LADDER — Register: escalating Steel
**The job:** Get paid without burning the relationship — until the relationship has proven it's already burned. Fixed-date ladder, never mood-based (Desk Law §8).
**The ladder:**
- **Day 1 past due — the assumption of innocence.** "The [month] invoice ([$X], due [date]) looks unpaid on my end — most of the time this is a card that rolled over or an inbox miss. Link's here: [link]. If it's already handled, ignore me."
- **Day 8 — the direct ask.** "Second note on the [month] invoice — [$X], now [N] days past due. Is there a problem on your end I should know about, or can this clear by Friday?"
- **Day 15 — consequences named, calmly.** "The [month] invoice is [N] days out. Per the agreement, work pauses at [day 21] until the account is current — I'd rather not, so tell me what's going on. If cash timing is the issue, say so and we'll set a date that's real."
- **Day 21+ — pause confirmed, path back stated.** Work paused in writing, exact amount to resume, exact restart timeline once cleared. No anger, no essay. **Steel** at full strength.
**Spike-watch:** Never apologize for invoicing ("sorry to chase!"). Never threaten anything the contract doesn't back. Never keep delivering while pretending the ladder isn't running — the pause is the leverage, and it only works if it's real.

## 12 · RENEWAL CONVERSATION — Register: Counsel with receipts
**The job:** Turn a term ending into a next phase. **Luke Cage builds the case** (the receipts, the health read, the next-phase offer) — Robbie turns the case into the email. The renewal motion starts BEFORE the final month, never inside it.
**The structure:** Open with their scoreboard, not the contract ("Here's what the last [N] months actually produced: [Luke Cage's numbers]") → the honest read, including what underperformed (credibility lives in the admission) → the next-phase shape: continue, upgrade, or restructure — recommended, not menu'd → the term mechanics stated plainly (any contracted annual uplift stated as fact, never apologized for — ⚠️ the 6% figure was Churlish contract language; verify Creative Impact's actual agreement terms before citing any uplift) → calibrated close.
**Skeleton:**
> Before the contract math, the actual scoreboard: [3 receipt lines — leads, revenue attributed, content produced, rankings — real numbers only].
>
> Honest read: [the thing that underperformed] didn't hit where I wanted it. Here's what changes about that in the next phase: [specific adjustment].
>
> My recommendation for the next term: [the shape — same tier, Market Domination upgrade, or restructure], starting [date]. [If the agreement includes an annual adjustment: "Per the agreement, the annual [N]% adjustment applies, so the monthly is [$X]." — verify CI's contract terms; never import Churlish's 6% by habit.]
>
> What would make the next six months a no-brainer instead of a decision?
**Spike-watch:** Never open with "your contract is expiring" — that frames it as an ending. Never hide the uplift or offer to waive it unprompted. If the client's wobble is relational rather than logical, Desk Law §9: that's a call.

## 13 · REFERRAL ASK — Register: Counsel, light
**The job:** Turn a happy client into a source — with an ask specific enough to act on. "Know anyone who needs marketing?" is a spike; nobody's brain retrieves anything from a question that broad.
**The timing:** Ride a win. The referral ask lands inside 72 hours of a result they were excited about — never inside a routine month, never alongside an invoice.
**The structure:** The win, named ("that [specific result] this week was the fun kind") → the specific portrait ("the person I'm looking for: a [industry] owner doing [revenue range] who's [the exact pain]") → the tiny ask (an intro, not a sale: "if one name came to mind just now, a two-line intro email is all it takes — I'll take it from there and make you look good for sending them").
**Spike-watch:** One portrait, not three. No referral-fee talk in the first ask (it cheapens the favor; compensation conversations come after a referral lands, if at all). If no name comes to them, plant it and drop it — the ask itself keeps you referable.

## 14 · TESTIMONIAL / CASE-STUDY ASK — Register: Counsel, light
**The job:** Capture proof while the win is hot. Proof is fleet ammunition — Falcon's Touch 1, the proposals, the site — and it only gets made in the 72-hour window after a result.
**The structure:** The result, in their numbers → the ask sized honestly ("three sentences, or a 10-minute recorded call if you're up for it — your words, zero polish needed") → make it effortless: offer to draft from their quick voice note for their approval → where it'll live, stated up front (permission baked into the ask, per Proof Law).
**Spike-watch:** Never send a blank "write us a review?" — always offer the draft-from-your-words path; approval-of-a-draft converts at multiples of write-from-scratch. Never use it anywhere they didn't approve. Log every yes where Professor X and the proposal generator can find it.

## 15 · SAYING NO / DECLINING / PARTING WAYS — Register: Steel, warm-handed
**The job:** Decline the project, the client, or the continued relationship — cleanly, fast, and without a justification essay that invites negotiation.
**The read:** Wrong-fit prospect (fast, kind, refer them onward if possible) vs. bad-fit project from a good client (protect the relationship, decline the work) vs. ending a client relationship (**call first** — Desk Law §9 — this email is the written confirmation).
**The structure:** The decision, first sentence, unhedged → one honest reason, one sentence, no essay (every extra sentence of justification is a handle to negotiate with) → the redirect: a referral, a different scope, or a clean goodbye → warmth in the close without reopening the door.
**Skeleton (wrong-fit prospect):**
> I'm going to pass on this one — after looking properly, [honest one-liner: the fit issue, stated without blame].
>
> Not a dead end though: [name/type of provider] does exactly this kind of work well, and I'm glad to make the intro.
>
> Glad you asked us first. Door's open if the [thing Creative Impact actually does] side ever becomes the priority.
**Spike-watch:** No "unfortunately, at this time, due to capacity..." throat-clearing. No fake maybes to soften it — a kind clean no today beats a resentful yes for six months. Parting-ways confirmations: dates, deliverable handoffs, and final invoicing stated as facts, zero editorializing.

## 16 · ESCALATION & PLATFORM DISPUTES — Register: Documented, fully
**The job:** Win against an institution — Meta, a platform, a vendor, a BBB process — where the reader is a caseworker with 90 seconds and a checklist. Personality loses here; completeness wins. The Creative Impact voice is suspended entirely, on purpose (Desk Law §6).
**The structure:**
- **Subject:** every reference number. "Ad Account [ID] — Appeal following [N] rejections — Case #[X]"
- **Paragraph 1:** who, what account/order, what's being requested, in three sentences.
- **The timeline:** dated event log, one line per event, oldest first. "March 3 — [event]. March 7 — [event]." Nothing editorialized; dates do the arguing.
- **The evidence:** attachments named and numbered in-text ("Attachment 2: rejection notice dated [date]"). Never "see attached."
- **The specific request:** exactly what resolution, by when, and the stated next step if unresolved ("absent resolution by [date], the complaint proceeds to [next venue]") — stated as procedure, not threat.
**The pre-flight (mandatory):** verify every date against the actual record before sending — one wrong date discredits the whole timeline (Fable Law 6 applied to receipts). Number the attachments. Have the next venue genuinely ready before naming it.
**Spike-watch:** Zero adjectives ("outrageous," "unacceptable" — delete on sight; they mark the letter as venting, not procedure). Zero sarcasm, however earned. Each escalation letter is self-contained — never "as I said in my previous seven appeals"; restate the record fresh, every time, because each reader is new.

---

**Cross-situation law:** when a live email spans two playbooks (a renewal email that must also handle a price objection; a reactivation that walks into an old unpaid invoice), run BOTH playbooks and let the harder register win the tone. Money conversations outrank warmth conversations when they collide — warmth survives honesty; it never survives evasion.
